# Naming Ratification — Self-Overflow Catastrophes

Second-pass naming review for the five new **self-overflow catastrophes** introduced by the concentration-additive self-craft rule (see open thread in conversation 2026-05-26).

When a Wood-pure entity at concentration ≥4 combines with another Wood-pure entity such that the summed concentration is ≥5, the result is a **Wood-overflow catastrophe** — a Storm-class entity with `phase_weights = {wood: 1.0}` (pure actor phase). Same construction applies for each of the other four phases.

This doc proposes 4 candidate names per slot and recommends one per slot. **Ratified 2026-05-26** — the five backup names were chosen over the primaries (see §Final names below). The primaries-and-backups structure is preserved as the audit trail; the final names override the recommendations in each slot.

## Criteria (carried forward from `naming-ratification.md`)

1. **Predict-your-next-drag** — the player sees a Wood (1) dropped on a Wind (4) and can reasonably guess "something even worse than Wind."
2. **One real English word** wherever possible.
3. **No conflict** with any other entity in the 95-entity v1 set. No substring collision.
4. **Readable at icon size.**

## Design constraints (carried forward)

- No clinical / anatomical vocabulary in the surface name.
- No substring reuse of the input names (so a Wood-overflow can't be called Woodstorm or Overgrowth-Storm).
- Must read as a *catastrophe*, distinct from the corresponding Storm (Wind, Heart, Mud, Drought, Frost — concentration 4) and from the corresponding insubordinate (Wildfire, Steam, Quicksand, etc.).

## Family-overlap check before authoring

The existing v1 set already has heavy clusters in two elemental families that the overflow names must avoid duplicating:

- **Fire family** (5 existing entries): Bonfire, Inferno, Wildfire, Firestorm, Conflagration. The Fire-overflow name must be precise enough not to read as a sixth Fire-noun.
- **Water family** (4 existing entries): Rain, Tide, Spring, Monsoon, plus the Water Storm **Frost**. The Water-overflow must not read as a fifth flood-word.

The other three families (Wood: Bramble, Gale, Wind; Earth: Erosion, Quicksand, Mud, Eruption; Metal: Rust, Axe, Forge, Patina) are less crowded.

---

## Slot 1 — Wood-overflow

Wood concentration ≥5 = the Liver Wind pattern past Storm. The image is uncontrolled vegetative force at landscape scale — a green tide, a runaway forest, the world overrun.

| Candidate | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **Verdance** | Single English word ("verdant" as a noun). Reads as overwhelming greenness. No conflict with the set. Captures the *positive-coded force gone wrong* register that Wood-Yang excess has in the source material. | Slightly literary; some players won't know the word. |
| **Wildwood** | Single word. Strong association with feral, untamed forest. Captures both the vegetative and the chaotic registers. | **Substring violation** — contains "Wood" literally. Disqualified. |
| **Riot** | Single word, dramatic, captures the liver-Wind/anger register of overflow. Real English noun for "uncontrolled outbreak." | Reads as social/political, not natural. Hard to draw an emoji for. |
| **Greenwild** | Captures Wood-at-large-scale. | Invented compound; violates the "real English word" rule. |
| **Thicket** | Real word for dense, impenetrable Wood. | Too small in scale — a thicket is a corner of a forest, not a catastrophe. |
| **Tanglewood** | Real word (and a place name), captures impenetrable vegetative chaos. | **Substring violation** — contains "Wood." Disqualified. |
| **Blight** | Single word. Captures vegetative force gone destructive (plant disease as overgrowth's failure mode). Has the dark/negative tone of a catastrophe. | Reads more as disease than force. The image is a *failing* forest, not an *overwhelming* one. |
| **Jungle** | Single word, vivid, captures vegetative excess at scale. | Reads more as biome than catastrophe. A jungle isn't a disaster the way a Firestorm is. |

**Recommendation: Verdance.**

The Wood family's existing entries (Bramble, Gale, Wind) all read as *narrow* or *kinetic* — a thorn-bush, a moving wind, the wind. None read as *mass*. Verdance covers the mass register that's missing and stays one word. The slight literary cost is worth the precision; **Conflagration** (a five-syllable Latinate already in the set) sets the precedent that the catastrophe row can carry a more bookish name when the image is right.

**Backup: Blight** if Verdance reads too gentle in playtest.

## Slot 2 — Fire-overflow

Fire concentration ≥5 = Heart Heat past Storm. The image is sustained heat at world scale — a sun, a continent on fire, the air itself burning. The hard part is finding a word that isn't already in the Fire family (Bonfire, Inferno, Wildfire, Firestorm, Conflagration).

| Candidate | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **Pyrocumulus** | Real meteorological term — the fire-fed thunderhead that forms over a megafire. Precise, distinct from every existing Fire entity, vivid. | Four syllables, technical. Player will not predict the word from inputs. |
| **Solarstorm** | Single compound (real astrophysical term), vivid, captures Fire at the largest possible scale. | Compound (violates "one real English word"). **Substring violation** — contains "Storm" (used as suffix in Firestorm, Sandstorm). |
| **Cinder** | Single English word. Real noun. | Too small in scale — a cinder is what's left *after* a fire, not the fire itself. Wrong tense. |
| **Brimstone** | Single recognizable word, real noun, captures sulfurous biblical-scale fire. Distinct phonetically from the other Fire entries. | Reads more as a *substance* than an *event*. Less catastrophic in tone than Firestorm. |
| **Pyre** | Single word. | **Already used** (cell #58, Dam + Kindling = Pyre). Disqualified. |
| **Smelter** | Single word, captures sustained Fire at industrial scale. | Reads as Metal-Forge family. Wrong element register. |
| **Heatwave** | Real compound, immediately readable, vivid, captures sustained Fire at landscape scale. | Compound (technically violates "one real English word") but is a recognized lexicalized unit. |
| **Roast** | Single word. | Too small / too domestic in scale. |
| **Pyresong** | Invented compound. | Disqualified — invented. |
| **Pyroclasm** | Real geological term (pyroclastic flow + cataclysm root). Single word in registers that allow it. | Borderline real — the standard form is "pyroclastic," not "pyroclasm." Marginal. |

**Recommendation: Pyrocumulus.**

The criterion the Fire-overflow has to clear is: distinct from Bonfire, Inferno, Wildfire, Firestorm, Conflagration. Every "fire" or "flame" word collides. Pyrocumulus does what Tundra did for the Metal-Water Storm pair — it picks a real, precise, slightly technical English word that the existing five Fire-nouns don't touch. The image (a fire so large it makes its own weather) is exactly right for "Fire past Storm."

**Backup: Heatwave** if Pyrocumulus reads too technical in playtest. Lexicalized compound, immediately predictable, very vivid.

## Slot 3 — Earth-overflow

Earth concentration ≥5 = Spleen Dampness past Storm. The image is earth at landscape scale — mountains moving, ground collapsing, mass-of-earth gone unstable. The Earth family is moderately crowded (Erosion, Quicksand, Mud, Eruption, Fissure) so the overflow must read as bigger than all of those.

| Candidate | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **Landslide** | Single recognized English word, immediately predictable, vivid, captures earth-at-scale-out-of-control. Distinct from Erosion (small-scale wear), Quicksand (insubordinate), Mud (Storm), Eruption (Heart + Mud catastrophe), Fissure (Mud + Drought catastrophe). | Compound (Land + slide) but lexicalized. |
| **Avalanche** | Single recognized word, dramatic, captures massive earth-or-snow movement. | Reads more as snow than earth in modern English. Some Earth/Water-Frost ambiguity. |
| **Mudslide** | Single word, vivid, predictable. | **Substring violation** — contains "Mud" (the Earth Storm). Disqualified. |
| **Quake** | Single word, the canonical short noun for earth in motion. Real English. Distinct from Eruption (vertical) — Quake is horizontal/shaking. | Slightly small-scale-coded (we say "earthquake" for the disaster, "quake" can read as the verb). |
| **Cataclysm** | Real word, catastrophic register, distinct from every existing entity. | Generic — could apply to any element. Doesn't specifically read as Earth. |
| **Crag** | Single word, real noun. | Reads as a *feature*, not a catastrophe. Wrong tense. |
| **Sinkhole** | Real compound, vivid, captures earth collapsing into itself. | Smaller in scale than the other catastrophes. Reads as point-source rather than landscape. |
| **Tectonic** | Real word, captures geological scale. | Adjective, not noun, in standard usage. |

**Recommendation: Landslide.**

Landslide is the cleanest read. It scales naturally — the player who has just dropped Wood on Wind doesn't have to think hard to predict that the Earth equivalent would be "the ground itself moving." It doesn't collide with any existing Earth-family member, doesn't substring any of them, and the compound is lexicalized enough to read as one word.

**Backup: Avalanche** if Landslide reads too quiet.

## Slot 4 — Metal-overflow

Metal concentration ≥5 = Lung Dryness past Storm. This is the slot with the most authoring difficulty because the Metal Storm is **Drought**, which is itself an absence-of-water concept, and the existing Metal family (Axe, Forge, Rust, Patina, Ore) is grouped around blade/oxidation/industrial vocabulary. The overflow must read as Drought-past-Drought, *not* a sharper blade.

| Candidate | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **Desolation** | Real word, single (longer) lexeme, captures the Metal-as-Drought register exactly — emptiness, dryness, scoured-out landscape. Distinct from every existing entity. | Four syllables, abstract. Less concrete than Firestorm. |
| **Wasteland** | Recognized lexicalized compound, vivid, captures the dried-out, mineral-stripped landscape of Metal excess. | Compound. |
| **Salt-Flat** | Real lexical unit. Captures the precise image — what Drought past Drought leaves behind. | Hyphenated; violates the rule. |
| **Bonemeal** | Vivid, real word, captures what Metal does at scale (reducing things to dry mineral residue). | Has agricultural / fertilizer connotations that confuse the catastrophe register. |
| **Aridity** | Real word, captures dryness-at-scale. | Reads as an abstract property, not an event. Wrong tense. |
| **Calcify** | Real word. | Verb, not a catastrophe-noun. |
| **Bonefield** | Invented compound. | Disqualified. |
| **Hailstorm** | Real lexical unit. | **Substring violation** — contains "Storm" (in Firestorm/Sandstorm pattern). Also reads as Water-Frost, not Metal. |
| **Scour** | Single word, captures Metal-as-abrasion at scale. | Verb in primary usage; less concrete than the others. |
| **Caldera** | Real word, dramatic, captures dried-volcanic-emptiness. | Reads as Fire (volcanic origin). Wrong element. |

**Recommendation: Desolation.**

The Metal Storm Drought already does the "dryness" work. Metal-overflow needs to escalate *past* Drought, and the natural escalation is "the landscape that Drought has left." Desolation carries that — it's the noun for "the state of being scoured out and emptied." It does not collide with any Metal entity (Axe, Forge, Rust, Patina, Ore, Drought) and pairs cleanly with the Storm-class register set by Tundra, Conflagration, and Permafrost (the existing longer-Latinate catastrophe names).

**Backup: Wasteland** if Desolation reads too abstract — Wasteland is more concrete and equally distinct from the existing Metal family.

## Slot 5 — Water-overflow

Water concentration ≥5 = Kidney Cold past Storm. The image is unbounded water at landscape scale — sea overrunning land, the deep, sustained inundation. The Water family is the second-most crowded (Rain, Tide, Spring, Monsoon, Frost, plus Wellspring, Spring-Water, Dew, Quench-Bath).

| Candidate | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **Deluge** | Single English word, the canonical Latinate for "massive flood" — Biblical scale. Distinct from Monsoon (regular weather), Tide (cyclic), Rain (small), Flooding (the Water Surge). | Slightly literary, but in the same register as Conflagration and Desolation. |
| **Maelstrom** | Single recognized word, dramatic, captures circular destructive water at scale. Real meteorological/oceanographic term. | Reads as whirlpool specifically, not flood. Image is rotational, not overwhelming. |
| **Tempest** | Single word, dramatic, real English. | Reads as storm/wind more than as water. Slightly Wood/Wind-coded. |
| **Tsunami** | Single recognized word, modern, immediately predictable as massive-water-catastrophe. | Reads as origin-event (earthquake-driven wave), not as sustained inundation. Also somewhat clinical/news-vocabulary. |
| **Inundation** | Real word, formally precise for "being overwhelmed by water." | Five syllables, abstract. Less vivid than Deluge. |
| **Cataract** | Real word — both "large waterfall" and "deluge" senses. Recognized English. | Polysemous (also means an eye condition). Some players will misread. |
| **Whelm** | Real archaic English word for "to overflow / submerge." | Too archaic. Most players won't recognize. |
| **Drown** | Single word, vivid. | Verb in primary usage. Reads as action, not as catastrophe-event. |
| **Watershed** | Real word, but specifically geographical / metaphorical. | **Substring violation** — contains "Water." Disqualified. |
| **Riptide** | Real compound. | Smaller in scale (a riptide is a current, not a flood). Also **substring violation** — contains "Tide." Disqualified. |

**Recommendation: Deluge.**

Deluge is the canonical English single-word noun for water-catastrophe-at-scale. It does not collide with any of the nine existing Water-family entries, is immediately predictable from the inputs, and sits comfortably in the same register as Conflagration (Heart-Drought) and Desolation (proposed Metal-overflow) — three Latinate single-word catastrophes that read as "biblical scale."

**Backup: Maelstrom** if Deluge feels too still — Maelstrom adds rotational drama, at the cost of a slightly less literal read.

## Final names (ratified 2026-05-26)

| Slot | Element | Self-overflow catastrophe |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Wood | **Blight** |
| 2 | Fire | **Heatwave** |
| 3 | Earth | **Avalanche** |
| 4 | Metal | **Wasteland** |
| 5 | Water | **Maelstrom** |

All five pass the four ratification criteria:
- Single English word or fully lexicalized compound.
- No substring collision with any of the 95 existing entities.
- No conceptual collision with any sibling in the same elemental family.
- Predictable as an escalation past the corresponding Storm.

**Tonal coherence**: the ratified set lands in the plain-English / recognized-disaster register (Blight, Heatwave, Avalanche, Wasteland, Maelstrom), pairing naturally with the existing Sandstorm, Dustbowl, Blizzard, Firestorm, Permafrost group. The Latinate catastrophes already in v1 (Conflagration, Tundra, Eruption, Geyser, Fissure) carry the more bookish register on their own; the new five do not need to match them. The two registers coexist by element family, not by phase pair, which mirrors how Wu Xing source material itself spans both vernacular and classical English translations.

## Phase weights (for completeness)

Each self-overflow catastrophe has `phase_weights = {own_phase: 1.0}`. This makes them mathematically distinct from the existing Storm × Storm catastrophes (which are 50/50 between the two parent phases). In Balance mode they will read as **the most efficient single move for unbalancing the pentagram in a single direction** — and therefore as the most expensive thing to produce in a puzzle, alongside the existing 50/50 catastrophes. This is consistent with the design intent of the new concentration mechanic: overflowing past Storm should feel decisive.
